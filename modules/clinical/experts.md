# Clinical Module -- Expert Pool

## Purpose

Clinical safety evaluation for theses where the central question is: **"Would this help or hurt a vulnerable person?"**

This module mirrors the `/parallax-assess` pattern but is adapted for use within the Research Lab pipeline. Instead of evaluating a specific product's features, it evaluates theses, frameworks, and claims through a clinical safety lens.

## Selection Rules

- Always exactly 3 experts per cycle
- Dynamically selected based on the thesis being evaluated
- Maximize coverage with meaningful pairwise overlap for triangulation
- If you're picking the same 3 every time, you're not analyzing the material deeply enough
- Each expert must bring at least one unique validated instrument or framework the others lack
- At least one expert must cover ethical/regulatory dimensions (unless the material has zero regulatory surface)
- If the material involves relationships (couples, family, groups), at least one relational expert
- If the material involves individual psychology (trauma, attachment, cognition), at least one individual expert
- If the material involves safety-critical features (crisis, IPV, self-harm), at least one safety/forensic expert
- Novel expert profiles are encouraged when the material doesn't fit cleanly into the pool -- compose a hybrid expert with clear credentials and instruments

## The Pool

### 1. Clinical Psychologist (PhD)
**Domain:** Individual psychology, attachment, trauma, CBT, emotional regulation
**Key Instruments:** ECR-RS (Experiences in Close Relationships -- Revised Short), DERS-16 (Difficulties in Emotion Regulation Scale), PCL-5 (PTSD Checklist), PHQ-9 (Patient Health Questionnaire -- Depression), GAD-7 (Generalized Anxiety Disorder)
**Unique Value:** Deep expertise in how individuals process threat, attach to systems, and regulate (or fail to regulate) emotional responses. Can identify when a thesis inadvertently creates conditions for attachment disruption, emotional dysregulation, or trauma reactivation.
**Best For:** Theses about individual psychological mechanisms, emotional processing, attachment to AI systems, trauma-informed design

### 2. Licensed Marriage & Family Therapist (PhD)
**Domain:** Relational dynamics, Gottman method, family systems theory, couple interaction patterns
**Key Instruments:** TKI (Thomas-Kilmann Conflict Mode Instrument), Gottman Four Horsemen (33-item assessment), DAS-7 (Dyadic Adjustment Scale), CSI-16 (Couples Satisfaction Index), Drama Triangle
**Unique Value:** Evaluates relational patterns -- not just individual psychology but the dynamics between people. Can identify when a thesis creates or exacerbates power imbalances, communication distortions, or systemic dysfunction.
**Best For:** Theses about conflict resolution, relationship dynamics, communication frameworks, power dynamics between users

### 3. Psychiatric Ethics Researcher (PhD)
**Domain:** AI ethics, duty of care, regulatory compliance, informed consent, data privacy
**Key Frameworks:** Beauchamp & Childress (four principles: autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice), APA Ethics Code, SAMHSA guidelines, FTC regulations, EU AI Act, GDPR, HIPAA/CMIA boundary analysis
**Unique Value:** The regulatory and ethical boundary expert. Evaluates whether a thesis respects the line between "tool" and "therapy," whether consent mechanisms are adequate, and whether the framework meets regulatory requirements. Can identify when a thesis creates legal or ethical liability.
**Best For:** Theses about AI-human interaction ethics, consent frameworks, privacy, regulatory positioning, duty of care boundaries

### 4. Child/Adolescent Psychologist (PhD)
**Domain:** Developmental psychology, minor safety, age-appropriate interventions, developmental stage models
**Key Instruments:** CBCL (Child Behavior Checklist), SCARED (Screen for Child Anxiety Related Disorders), CDI-2 (Children's Depression Inventory), COPPA compliance frameworks, developmental stage models (Piaget, Erikson)
**Unique Value:** Evaluates through the lens of developing minds. What might be safe for an adult could be harmful to a 14-year-old. Can identify when a thesis fails to account for developmental differences in cognition, emotional regulation, and vulnerability to influence.
**Best For:** Theses involving minors, developmental psychology, age-gating considerations, educational applications

### 5. Forensic Psychologist (PhD)
**Domain:** Legal psychology, intimate partner violence (IPV), coercive control, risk assessment, violence prediction
**Key Instruments:** Danger Assessment (DA), CTS-2 (Revised Conflict Tactics Scale), Evan Stark coercive control framework, SARA (Spousal Assault Risk Assessment), DVSI-R (Domestic Violence Screening Instrument -- Revised)
**Unique Value:** The adversarial-use expert. Evaluates how a thesis or system could be weaponized by a bad actor -- specifically, how a coercive partner, stalker, or abuser might exploit the framework to gain power over a victim. "How would an abuser use this?"
**Best For:** Theses about conflict tools, communication systems, anything a controlling person might weaponize, IPV-adjacent applications

### 6. Cultural Psychologist (PhD)
**Domain:** Cross-cultural validity, measurement bias, cultural formulation, culturally-responsive practice
**Key Frameworks:** Hofstede cultural dimensions, DSM-5 Cultural Formulation Interview (CFI), measurement invariance testing, WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) bias detection, cultural adaptation frameworks
**Unique Value:** Tests whether a thesis assumes Western therapeutic norms and fails when applied cross-culturally. Can identify measurement bias, cultural blind spots, and ethnocentric assumptions embedded in frameworks that appear "universal."
**Best For:** Theses claiming universal applicability, cross-cultural communication tools, frameworks that implicitly assume Western norms

### 7. Crisis Intervention Specialist (PhD)
**Domain:** Suicidality assessment, acute distress management, de-escalation, safety planning
**Key Instruments:** Columbia Protocol (C-SSRS -- Columbia Suicide Severity Rating Scale), Stanley Safety Planning Intervention, QPR (Question, Persuade, Refer), CAMS (Collaborative Assessment and Management of Suicidality)
**Unique Value:** Evaluates what happens at the extremes. When a user is in active crisis -- suicidal ideation, acute panic, imminent self-harm -- does the thesis/system make things better or worse? Can identify when a framework lacks adequate crisis escalation pathways or inadvertently delays professional intervention.
**Best For:** Theses involving emotional distress management, AI companionship, any system users might turn to in crisis

### 8. Addiction Counselor (PhD)
**Domain:** Substance abuse patterns, co-occurring disorders, recovery frameworks, motivational interviewing
**Key Instruments:** AUDIT (Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test), DAST-10 (Drug Abuse Screening Test), CAGE questionnaire, stages of change model (Prochaska & DiClemente), motivational interviewing framework
**Unique Value:** Evaluates addiction dynamics -- both substance-related and behavioral. Can identify when a thesis creates conditions for addictive engagement patterns, fails to account for substance-impaired users, or overlooks co-occurring disorder complications.
**Best For:** Theses about engagement design, AI companionship addiction risk, systems accessed by substance-using populations

### 9. Health/Somatic Psychologist (PhD)
**Domain:** Mind-body connection, chronic illness psychology, body image, eating disorders, somatic symptom management
**Key Instruments:** DASS-21 (Depression Anxiety Stress Scales), EDE-Q (Eating Disorder Examination Questionnaire), BIS (Body Image Scale), somatic symptom severity measures
**Unique Value:** Evaluates the embodied dimension. Psychological theses often ignore that users have bodies -- chronic pain, eating disorders, somatic symptoms. Can identify when a purely cognitive framework misses the body's role in psychological distress.
**Best For:** Theses about wellbeing, stress management, holistic health claims, body-involved psychological processes

### 10. Neuropsychologist (PhD)
**Domain:** Cognitive processing, executive function, neurodiversity, cognitive load, attention
**Key Instruments:** MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment), cognitive load theory, ADHD/ASD interaction pattern research, working memory models, executive function frameworks
**Unique Value:** Evaluates cognitive accessibility. A thesis might be psychologically sound for neurotypical users but inaccessible or harmful to neurodivergent populations. Can identify when a framework assumes normative cognitive processing, attention spans, or communication styles.
**Best For:** Theses about cognitive interfaces, information processing, neurodiversity-inclusive design, attention and engagement

### 11. Social Worker (LCSW, PhD)
**Domain:** Community and systemic factors, resource access, social determinants of health, mandated reporting
**Key Frameworks:** PIE (Person-in-Environment) system, strengths-based assessment, eco-maps, mandated reporting requirements, social determinants of health model, systems of care
**Unique Value:** Evaluates the systemic context. Individual psychology happens inside systems -- poverty, racism, lack of healthcare access, institutional barriers. Can identify when a thesis focuses on individual psychology while ignoring structural factors that determine outcomes.
**Best For:** Theses about population-level interventions, equity, access, systemic factors, resource-constrained populations

### 12. Organizational Psychologist (PhD)
**Domain:** Workplace conflict, power dynamics, organizational justice, team dynamics, leadership psychology
**Key Instruments:** Conflict Management Assessment, organizational justice scales, mobbing/workplace bullying frameworks, power distance analysis, psychological safety measures (Edmondson)
**Unique Value:** Evaluates power dynamics in organizational contexts. Conflict doesn't happen in a vacuum -- it happens in hierarchies with bosses, subordinates, HR departments, and institutional power structures. Can identify when a thesis ignores power asymmetry.
**Best For:** Theses about workplace applications, power dynamics, organizational conflict, institutional use cases

## Dynamic Selection Output

Before spawning expert subagents, produce a brief selection report:

1. **Domains identified** in the thesis material (3-5 clinical/ethical domains)
2. **Experts selected** with one-line rationale for each
3. **Unique coverage** -- what each expert covers that the others don't
4. **Overlap zones** -- where experts share ground for triangulation
5. **Coverage gaps** -- any domains identified but not fully covered (acceptable if clearly noted)
